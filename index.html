The Chemistry Behind Airbags

Brief History of Airbags

Airbags date back to the early 1950s. In 1952, John Hetrick, an inventor from Detroit, Michigan, patented the first airbag design. Hetrick's design used a chemical reaction to inflate the airbag, but it was never put into production. In the 1960s, Mercedes-Benz began developing airbags for its vehicles. The first Mercedes-Benz with airbags was the 1971 W116 S-Class, however airbags did not become widely available until the 1980s. Today airbags are standard equipment on most vehicles, they have been credited with saving thousands of lives. The first airbags were actually used in fighter jets in the 1950s. It wasn't until the 1970s when airbags began to be used in cars.

Airbags are a safety feature in vehicles that are designed to protect the driver and passengers in the event of a collision. They work by rapidly inflating a bag of nylon or another material that is located in the steering wheel, dashboard, or side of the vehicle. The airbag then deploys in front of the driver or passenger, cushioning their impact and reducing their risk of injury.

How's Chemistry Involved?

Although most people do not associate chemistry with cars, a great deal of chemistry occurs in them. This can range from the burning of gasoline to power the engine to chemical reactions in the battery to generate electricity. Another reaction that occurs that many people take for granted is the airbag. Airbags are inflated not by compressed gas but by the byproducts of a chemical reaction. Sodium azide, also known as NaN3, is a chemical that is primarily used in airbags It's a white, crystalline solid that, when heated, decomposes rapidly into sodium metal and nitrogen gas.

Notice that the second product of the above reaction is N2 which is also known as nitrogen gas. A handful (130 grams) of sodium azide will produce 67 liters of nitrogen gas which is enough to inflate a normal airbag.

The amount of sodium azide in an airbag is critical to its proper functioning. Too little sodium azide and the airbag will not inflate properly. Too much sodium azide and the airbag could deploy with too much force causing more harm to the driver or passenger. So basically, if the amount of sodium azide in an airbag is increased, the airbag will deploy in more force, and if the amount of sodium azide in an airbag is decreased the airbag will not deploy with enough force and reduce the effectiveness of the airbag

When the airbag deploys, the sodium metal byproduct of the sodium azide is mixed with a small amount of potassium nitrate, which helps to slow the decomposition of the sodium azide and ensure that the airbag inflates slowly enough to protect the driver and passengers. Some airbags also use silicon dioxide, which, along with potassium nitrate, reacts with the sodium metal to form potassium silicate and sodium silicate, both of which prevent the sodium from reacting with moisture in the air to form corrosive sodium hydroxide, which can harm the eyes and skin.

It is worth noting that the other chemical into which sodium azide degrades is Na, also known as sodium. Because sodium is a highly reactive metal that quickly reacts with water to form sodium hydroxide, it would be extremely dangerous if it got into your eyes, nose, or mouth. To reduce the risk of exposure, airbag manufacturers combine sodium azide with other chemicals that react with the sodium to produce less toxic compounds.

What causes an airbag to inflate as a result of this reaction? They are sensors in the front of the vehicle that detect collisions. These sensors transmit an electric signal to the canister containing the sodium azide, which detonates a small amount of an igniter compound. The heat generated by the ignition begins the decomposition of the sodium azide and the production of nitrogen to fill the airbag. What's amazing about this is that the time between when the sensor detects a collision and when the airbag is fully inflated is only about 30 milliseconds, or 0.03 of a second. About 50 milliseconds after an accident, the car's occupant collides with the airbag, which deflates and absorbs the occupant's forward motion.
